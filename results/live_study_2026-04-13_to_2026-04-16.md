# Live Study: April 13-16, 2026

## Scope

- Sessions reviewed: Monday, April 13, 2026 through Thursday, April 16, 2026
- Sources: `logs/2026-04-13` through `logs/2026-04-16`, `bot_history.db`
- Strategy in `config/live_config.json`: 15-symbol mean reversion, 15-minute bars, static threshold mode, SMA exits, ATR percentile cap enabled at `80.0`

## Method Notes

- `daily_report.py` was used for the day-level diagnostics.
- `order_history` in `bot_history.db` was used for actual filled-order timelines because `observed_at_utc` re-observes open orders after submission.
- `positions.jsonl` contains repeated lifecycle snapshots for some carried positions. For symbol-level trade review, duplicate records were collapsed by `(trace, event)` and interpreted conservatively.
- Monday clearly started with inventory already on the books from prior activity, so some Monday opens/closes reflect carryover rather than fresh same-day round trips.

## Executive Summary

- The bot was active all four sessions and evaluated `1,485` bars across the 15-symbol universe.
- Across the four days it produced `50` buy signals and `16` sell signals, but only `21` submitted orders became live fills during the period.
- Closed-trade PnL from the position-close logs was approximately `+$16.88`.
- Account snapshots were less encouraging than the closed-trade tally: the latest `daily_pnl` snapshot was positive on Monday, flat on Tuesday, negative on Wednesday, and negative again on Thursday.
- The biggest operational problem was feed freshness on Tuesday and Thursday. The biggest trading problem was low signal conversion outside Monday.

## Entry And Exit Timing Review

### What the timing looked like

- Fresh entries clustered mostly in the middle of the regular session:
  - Monday: roughly `15:00` to `18:15 UTC`
  - Tuesday: one entry at `16:45 UTC`
  - Wednesday: entries at `15:00` and `17:03 UTC`
  - Thursday: entries from `14:43` to `16:45 UTC`
- Most logged exits were very short-hold sell-signal exits.

### Holding-time pattern

- Monday average closed holding time was short, with many closes effectively immediate or within one bar.
- Wednesday's winning `ABBV` trade also exited quickly.
- Thursday's only realized close, `HD`, held about `60.5` minutes and still exited flat.

### What this suggests

- The live system is not letting the average trade develop anywhere close to the backtest expectation of about `4.2` bars.
- A large share of realized outcomes were flat or near-flat exits, which is consistent with a strategy repeatedly entering and then immediately flattening on the next qualifying sell signal.
- The best realized names were the few that got enough room to move at all: `ABBV` and `AMD`.

## Stale-Bar Impact Analysis

### What actually happened

- Tuesday logged one clearly stale startup bar:
  - `ABBV` at `2026-04-14T13:15:01+00:00`
  - bar timestamp was still `2026-04-13T19:45:00+00:00`
  - bar age `62,100s`
- Thursday logged three stale startup bars for `ABBV`:
  - `2026-04-16T13:00:01+00:00`
  - `2026-04-16T13:15:01+00:00`
  - `2026-04-16T13:33:57+00:00`
  - all were still carrying the prior day's `19:45 UTC` bar

### Why this matters even if trades were later

- The stale startup bars themselves were not directly adjacent to the actual Tuesday trade or most Thursday trades.
- But the session-level averages still came in at `294.3s` on Tuesday and `363.2s` on Thursday.
- That means the issue is broader than just the overnight stale warnings: the live decision loop appears to have been operating with near-delay-threshold bars during those sessions.

### Trading impact interpretation

- Tuesday's near-zero activity is consistent with stale or delayed data suppressing otherwise valid opportunities.
- Thursday's activity recovered, but fill quality turned mixed and the day still ended with negative account-level daily PnL.
- In short: stale startup bars were the visible symptom; slow bar freshness across the session was the more important operational risk.

## Why So Many Signals Did Not Turn Into Trades

Examples from the logs:

- Monday:
  - `LLY`: `12` buy signals, `0` buy orders
  - `TSLA`: `10` buy signals, `0` buy orders
  - `AMD`: `4` buy signals, `1` buy order
- Thursday:
  - `HON`: `6` buy signals, `1` buy order

Risk logs explain part of this:

- Monday buy blocks included:
  - `max_open_positions_reached`: `9`
  - `stale_live_price`: `2`
  - `insufficient_buying_power`: `1`
  - `outside_entry_window`: `1`
- Thursday included `stale_live_price` blocks on both buy and sell sides.

Interpretation:

- The strategy may be generating enough candidate signals, but portfolio/risk constraints are preventing many of the best-looking names from actually entering.
- That means the live book is not sampling the same opportunity set as the backtest.

## Recommendations Before Friday

1. Fix feed freshness before studying parameter tweaks.
   The Tuesday and Thursday sessions are not clean enough to trust for strategy evaluation. The first priority is to reduce effective bar age and eliminate overnight stale bars at startup.

2. Add a startup guard that refuses to trade until all symbols have a same-session bar.
   The current stale warning is visible in the logs, but the bot still proceeds through startup with overnight data in the stream.

3. Measure signal-to-order drop-off explicitly.
   The live book is rejecting too many signals to compare fairly with research. Capture, per symbol and per session, whether each signal was blocked by position limits, buying power, stale price, or timing rules.

4. Review the `max_open_positions_reached` policy against the backtest assumptions.
   Monday in particular suggests the risk layer is heavily shaping which names get traded.

5. Review exit sensitivity for the live mean-reversion logic.
   Too many realized trades were flat or almost immediately reversed. That may be acceptable if the risk model intends it, but it does not look like the backtest's average holding profile.

6. Separate carryover inventory from same-day trading in the dashboard and reports.
   Monday's logs mix inherited positions with fresh live activity, which makes day-one analysis harder than it should be.

## Bottom Line

- The bot was alive and making decisions all four days.
- The best realized live contributors were `ABBV` and `AMD`.
- The biggest blocker to trustworthy evaluation is not raw PnL; it is data freshness plus heavy signal attrition between evaluation and execution.
- If Friday is meant to be a real validation day, fix bar freshness first, then re-evaluate signal conversion and holding behavior.
